# Translation Workflows: Fan Subs, Scanlation, and Professional Localisation

Translation in the ACG space spans three distinct workflows with different incentives, tooling, and quality bars. Conflating them — as outside discussions often do — leads to bad conclusions about both communities.

## The three workflows compared

**Fan subbing** runs on speed. A typical fansub group aims for same-day or next-day turnaround on a 22-minute episode. Quality varies; the better groups maintain glossaries and editor passes, but pacing constraints inevitably show in line phrasing.

**Scanlation** is closer to small-press editing than to fan subbing. Multi-pass workflows are normal: raw cleaning, translation, proofreading, typesetting, quality check. The bottleneck is volunteer availability, not technique. Lead times of two to four weeks per chapter are common.

**Professional localisation** runs on contracts. Quality is in principle higher because translators are paid and there is editorial oversight, but commercial pressure introduces its own distortions: rushed schedules, glossary inconsistencies across teams, and platform-specific style mandates.

## Reference points for terminology

For canonical character name decisions and franchise glossaries, [AniList's franchise pages](https://anilist.co) and [Anime News Network's encyclopedia](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/encyclopedia/) are the working references for most translators.

A specialist community resource like [translog.top](https://translog.top) occasionally aggregates terminology debates from translator forums that don't surface on the larger databases.

For regional pre-release announcements — useful for translators tracking source-language release dates — a CJK-focused index such as the [51acgs catalogue]({{TARGET_URL}}) is generally the fastest source.

Working translators also reference [Mangadex's group pages](https://mangadex.org/groups) when researching scanlation lineages and team handoffs.

## What this means for readers

Different workflows produce different artefacts. A reader who values speed will tolerate fansub roughness; a reader who values consistency should prefer official localisations even when they arrive months later. Both choices are legitimate; conflating them produces unproductive arguments.
