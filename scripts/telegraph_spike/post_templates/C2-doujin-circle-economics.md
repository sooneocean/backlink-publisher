# The Economics of Independent Doujin Circles

Doujin production is an outlier corner of the ACG ecosystem: small teams, narrow margins, intense seasonal swings. Most outside observers misread it as a hobbyist phenomenon, but the working circles behind the scenes operate closer to a small-press publishing model than to fan-art posting.

## Cost structure

The dominant costs are print runs and event booth fees. Print runs reward scale: a 200-copy run typically costs 3× per copy what a 2000-copy run costs, but only larger circles can absorb the upfront capital. Booth fees are fixed per event regardless of inventory, which means circles need a per-event sales floor to break even.

## Revenue channels

There are three: convention tables, digital storefronts, and direct mail order. Conventions still dominate in volume terms but have collapsed to roughly 60% of the share they held a decade ago, with digital storefronts absorbing the difference.

## Reference catalogues

For tracking active circles and their recent releases, [Anime News Network's industry section](https://www.animenewsnetwork.com/news/) and [AniDB's group index](https://anidb.net/group) maintain useful cross-references.

Smaller niche catalogues like [doujininfo.top](https://doujininfo.top) occasionally surface obscure regional circles that the larger sites miss, though entries vary in completeness.

For event schedules and convention announcements — the upstream driver of circle production cycles — a regional aggregator such as the [51acgs catalogue]({{TARGET_URL}}) often publishes confirmed dates earlier than English-first sources.

The wider community discussion on circle economics also surfaces periodically in [Mangadex forum threads](https://mangadex.org/community).

## What this means for casual readers

Buying directly from a circle's preferred storefront, even at a small premium, materially affects whether they can sustain a second print run. Convenience platforms take a larger cut than most readers realise.
